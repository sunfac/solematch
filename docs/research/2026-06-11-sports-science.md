# Research Dossier 1: Sports Science of Running Shoe Selection
*Researched 11 June 2026. Confidence levels: **Strong** (multiple RCTs/meta-analysis), **Moderate** (1 large RCT or consistent lab evidence), **Weak** (cohort/case-series/indirect), **Expert-opinion** (mechanistic reasoning, no direct trial).*

## (A) Key Findings with Citations & Effect Sizes

### 1. Shoe mass & running economy
- **Hoogkamer, Kram et al. 2016** (*Med Sci Sports Exerc*): adding mass raises metabolic cost ~**1% per 100 g per shoe**. In 18 sub-20-min 5k men, 3000 m time-trial slowed **0.65% (+100 g)** and **2.37% (+300 g)**; linear **0.78% per 100 g/shoe**. *(Strong)*
- **Rodrigo-Carranza et al. 2020** (*Frontiers in Physiology*): corroborates mass→economy penalty in trained runners. *(Strong)*
- Implication: mass matters, but a carbon-plate supershoe (often heavier than a flat) can still net positive because foam/plate gains exceed the mass penalty.

### 2. Carbon-plated "supershoes"
- **Hoogkamer et al. 2018** (*Sports Medicine*, Vaporfly prototype): mean **−4% metabolic cost** vs. established racing flats; **18/18 runners improved** (range ~2–6%) at all three speeds. Origin of the "4%" name. *(Strong)*
- **Kobayashi et al. 2026** (*Frontiers in Sports & Active Living*, meta-analysis, 14 crossover trials, n=271): pooled **running economy −2.88%** (95% CI −4.57 to −1.19), metabolic cost −2.64%, VO₂ −2.84%; mean improvement −2.75%. Certainty *moderate*. Only 3/14 studies included women. *(Strong for "benefit exists ~2.5–3%"; Moderate on magnitude)*
- **Individual variability is large and real**: 2023 meta-analytic data report responses **+9.7% to −1.1%** (amateurs +9.3% to −1.1%; elites +11.4% to −11.3%). Some runners are non- or negative-responders. *(Strong — variability is consistent)*
- **Benefit depends on speed — Dominy & Joubert 2022** (Vaporfly Next% 2 vs. Asics Hyperspeed): **−1.4%** VO₂ at 12 km/h (~3:30 marathon pace, p<0.001) but only **−0.9% (NS, p=0.065)** at 10 km/h (~4:12 pace) — vs. 2.7–4.2% at 14–18 km/h in elites. Benefit shrinks at slower speeds; near-zero for very slow runners. *(Moderate)*
- **Marathon/elite performance**: top-100 elite seasonal bests improved 0.6–2.3% across 10k/half/full since the AFT era; elite marathon ~0.68% when switching to AFT. All recent world records set in supershoes. *(Strong for elites; Moderate translation to amateurs)*
- **Women vs. men**: understudied. Both sexes benefit; no robust evidence of different response. *(Weak/Unknown)*

### 3. Midsole foams: PEBA vs. EVA vs. TPU
- Lab resilience/energy return: **PEBA ~87%** vs. **EVA/TPU ~40–52%**; best modern midsoles ~80%. PEBA is lightest with highest return but costlier and less durable (packs out faster) → race/flagship. **TPU** is springier and far more durable/temperature-stable than EVA → daily/long-run. **EVA** is cheap, comfortable, consistent, compresses fastest. Supercritical-foaming improves all. PEBA↔carbon-plate synergy confirmed in 2023–2024 bending-stiffness × foam studies. *(Strong on lab energy return; Moderate on "who benefits")*

### 4. Heel-toe drop & injury
- **Malisoux et al. 2016** (*Am J Sports Med*, RCT, 553 runners, 0/6/10 mm drop, 6-mo): **no overall difference** in injury rate by drop. Interaction with experience: low-drop **protective for occasional/novice** but **higher injury risk in regular/experienced** runners. *(Moderate)*
- **Malisoux et al. 2017** (adaptation RCT): running pattern only partially adapts to drop over 6 months → change drop gradually. *(Moderate)*

### 5. Maximal cushioning vs. traditional
- **Kulmala et al. 2018** (*Scientific Reports*): maximal shoes increase leg stiffness and amplify impact — impact peak **+10.7%**, loading rate **+12.3%** at 14.5 km/h (+6.4% peak at 10 km/h). The "cushioning paradox." *(Moderate)*
- **Hannigan & Pollard 2019** (*Am J Sports Med*) + Pollard et al. 2018: 6-week transition to maximal (Hoka) shoes did NOT reduce loading rate/impact peak and altered frontal-plane mechanics (greater peak eversion). *(Moderate)*
- **Malisoux et al. 2020** (*Am J Sports Med*, RCT, n=848): softer midsole → LOWER injury risk; hard version sub-hazard ratio **1.52 (1.07–2.16)**. *(Moderate)*

### 6. Comfort filter / preferred movement path
- **Nigg, Baltich et al. 2015** (*Br J Sports Med*): "preferred movement path" + "comfort filter" framework. *(Influential framework)*
- **Current standing: largely unvalidated for injury.** Secondary analysis of the Malisoux cohort (*Scand J Med Sci Sports* 2025) found perceived cushioning/comfort weakly or not associated with injury risk. Reviews (2020, 2021/2022) find no compelling evidence any prescription method prevents injury. Comfort remains defensible for adherence and economy. *(Weak / Expert-opinion)*
- Counterpoint: Willwacher et al. 2020 — running outside habitual joint path → greater knee-cartilage volume loss after 75 min (indirect support). *(Weak)*

### 7. Shoe rotation (parallel use)
- **Malisoux et al. 2015** (*Scand J Med Sci Sports*, prospective, 264 runners, 22 wk): >1 pair in parallel → hazard ratio **0.614 (95% CI 0.389–0.969)** ≈ **39% lower injury rate**. Mechanism: varied geometry distributes load across tissues. *(Moderate — single prospective cohort)*
- Newer literature mechanistic/expert-opinion, reinforcing; no contradicting RCT.

### 8. Pronation control / stability paradigm
- **Nielsen et al. 2014** (*Br J Sports Med*, 927 novices, neutral shoe, 1 yr): pronation NOT associated with higher injury; mild pronators had FEWER injuries per 1000 km. *(Strong against "pronation = injury")*
- **2022 systematic review** (12 RCTs, n=11,240): no reduction in injury from matching shoe type to runner. *(Strong against blanket prescription)*
- Narrow exception — **Willwacher/Malisoux 2021** (*JOSPT*): motion-control shoes reduced pronation-related injuries specifically (subgroup). *(Weak)*

### 9. Body weight / BMI & cushioning
- **Malisoux et al. 2020** (n=848): heavier runners did NOT gain extra protection from soft cushioning — soft-shoe benefit significant only for LIGHTER runners (light: hard-shoe SHR **1.80, 1.09–2.98**; heavy: SHR 1.23, NS). Body mass alone not associated with injury (SHR 1.00). **Refutes "heavier runners need maximal/soft cushioning to avoid injury."** *(Moderate)*
- Foam bottoming-out / higher-durometer needs for heavy runners = brand/anecdotal (durability and ride feel yes; injury no). *(Weak / Expert-opinion)*

### 10. Age / masters runners
- Aging → reduced gastrocnemius volume/fascicle length, lower ankle plantarflexor/propulsive power, distal-to-proximal load redistribution. Strength work recommended. *(Moderate biomechanics)*
- Rocker geometry + bending stiffness can offload weakening ankle/calf; AFT can reduce some RRI biomechanical factors without necessarily increasing Achilles load (2024–2025). "Masters need more cushion/rocker" is plausible, not RCT-proven. *(Weak / Expert-opinion)*

### 11. Sex differences
- **Last/fit**: women average narrower heel, relatively wider forefoot, higher arch, larger Q-angle → female-specific lasts. *(Moderate — anatomical)*
- **Epidemiology**: bone stress injuries **1.8–2.3× more frequent in women**; RED-S / low energy availability is the dominant driver (symptomatic LEA ~4.5× injury rate). Footwear secondary. *(Strong epi; Weak footwear link)*
- Supershoe response in women: understudied. *(Unknown)*

### 12. Supershoes for training/recovery — injury concern
- **Tenforde, Hoenig, Saxena, Hollander 2023** (*Sports Medicine*): case series of 5 competitive runners with **navicular bone-stress injuries** linked to carbon-fibre-plate shoes — 4 used CFP in training. Mechanism: rigid plate reduces MTP flexion, shifts load to midfoot; navicular = low-blood-supply bottleneck. Recommendations: gradual transition, monitor midfoot pain, cap volume. *(Weak — case series; plausible mechanism)*
- 2024 collegiate biomechanics: supershoes increased maximum eversion excursion. Practical consensus: cap supershoe volume (race + key sessions). *(Expert-opinion)*

### 13. Footwear periodisation by intent
- No RCT on full periodisation; coaching/clinical consensus + rotation evidence support: race = plate + PEBA high stack; tempo/long = super-trainer; easy/zone-2/daily = durable EVA/TPU moderate stack no plate; recovery = max-cushion comfort. Rotation across geometries varies tissue load and lets foams decompress. *(Rotation: Moderate; intent-mapping: Expert-opinion)*

## (C) What the Science Does NOT Support (excluded from algorithm)
1. Match shoe to foot type/pronation to prevent injury — refuted (Nielsen 2014; 2022 review).
2. Heavier runners need maximal/soft cushioning — refuted (Malisoux 2020).
3. More cushioning = less impact — backwards at speed (Kulmala 2018).
4. Comfortable shoe = injury-proof — unproven (2025 secondary analysis).
5. Lower drop safer for everyone — no (Malisoux 2016 interaction).
6. Supershoes make everyone ~4% faster — individual, pace-dependent.
7. Supershoes fine as everyday trainers — caution (Tenforde 2023).
8. Women respond same/differently to supershoes — genuinely unknown; fit differs.
9. The right shoe prevents running injuries — no prescription method reliably reduces injury; training load management outweighs shoe choice.

## Key caveats
1. Supershoe meta-evidence is overwhelmingly male and treadmill-based — flag amateur/female/outdoor extrapolation as lower-confidence.
2. Injury-prevention claims for ANY shoe attribute are weak — position the app as optimising performance/comfort/fit and managing load via rotation.
3. The most evidence-backed injury-relevant lever: **rotation** (Malisoux 2015). Strongest performance lever: **plate shoe for faster runners on race days** (Hoogkamer 2018; Kobayashi 2026).
